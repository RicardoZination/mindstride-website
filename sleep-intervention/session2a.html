The Problem with Too Much Time in Bed

Sleeping: it's natural to think that lying in bed longer will increase our chances of falling asleep. But here's the twist: staying in bed too much can actually lead to lighter, more fragmented sleep. That's where sleep restriction comes in—it's like a training program for your sleep patterns.

Understanding Sleep Restriction

Sleep restriction isn't about reducing the total amount of sleep you get. 

Instead, it's about spending the right amount of time in bed, so every moment counts towards deep, restorative sleep. 

It strengthens the bond between bed and sleep, making your sleep more efficient.

Implementing the Sleep Restriction Technique

By reducing the time you spend in bed to closely match the time you actually sleep, you naturally increase your body's need for sleep. This mild form of sleep deprivation means you'll feel sleepier in the evenings, which:

Setting Your Sleep Window

To start sleep restriction, you first determine your "sleep window" — the period during the night when you're actually sleeping. This is calculated from the sleep journal you are keeping, noting the average amount of sleep you get each night over two weeks. Your sleep window then becomes the fixed time you aim to go to bed and the time you should wake up.​

Strides for Sleep Restriction

Starting the Sleep Window: Don't go to bed before your sleep window begins, even if you're tired.

Falling Asleep: If you're not sleepy at the start of your sleep window, wait until you feel tired before going to bed.

Waking Up: The end of your sleep window is your fixed wake-up time. Get out of bed at this time, even if you feel like you could sleep more.

By following these steps, sleep restriction can significantly improve your sleep quality, helping you feel more energized during the day.

Adjusting Your Sleep Window

Initially, you might feel more tired, but that's your body adjusting. You'll monitor your sleep efficiency—the time you spend asleep versus the total time in bed. If you're not sleeping well, you'll shorten the sleep window a bit more.

As sleep improves, you can gradually extend it.

The Role of Sleep "Efficiency"

Sleep efficiency measures the percentage of time you spend asleep compared to the total time you spend in bed.  

You aim for about 80-85%, which means you're sleeping soundly for most of the time you're in bed.

It's a sign that your body is getting the rest it needs within the timeframe you've set.

Patience and Adjustment

The process requires patience. Sleep restriction can be challenging at first, but it's essential to stick with it. Over time, you'll find the perfect balance that lets you wake up feeling refreshed and alert.
