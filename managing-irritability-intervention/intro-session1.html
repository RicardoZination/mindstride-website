Introduction

When you're irritated, you may become easily upset or frustrated. Often, irritability feels like an overreaction to a situation. You might notice your reaction is more intense than necessary, or someone may have pointed it out to you (hopefully in a constructive way).

Feelings of agitation, frustration, and impatience are all signs of irritability. This can negatively impact both your personal and professional life. Throughout this intervention, we will explore what irritability is and how it presents itself in your body. We'll provide strategies to help you manage and reduce irritability, using self-identifying tools and resources to improve your mental health and overall well-being.

Welcome to "Strides for Managing Irritability," a science-based program designed to help you navigate and express strong feelings of irritability in a healthy way. Whether used together or individually, these tools and resources will increase your knowledge and ability to process feelings of frustration during stressful situations, improving your thinking patterns and social connections.

Managing and reducing feelings of irritation is a skill that can be developed over time with practice and patience. Throughout this intervention, you will learn about your emotional intelligence and receive tips to improve daily habits, reduce irritability, and identify effective communication styles for both personal and professional life.

Understanding Irritability?

According to the American Psychological Association, irritability involves excessive and easily triggered anger, annoyance, or impatience. People experiencing irritability tend to be impatient and short-tempered, quickly reacting with frustration to even minor obstacles.

While irritability is a natural human emotion, experiencing it in extremes may indicate an underlying issue that needs to be treated. Persistent and disruptive episodes of irritability can be symptoms of mental health conditions like anxiety or depression, or physical issues such as traumatic brain injury or substance abuse withdrawal.

Multifactorial Causes of Irritability

There is no singular trigger for irritability; its causes can be multifactorial, acute, or chronic.

Occasional Irritability:

  • Can stem from common disruptions like arguments, financial concerns, or work deadlines.

Chronic Irritability:

  • May result from psychological and physiological factors.

Irritability in Children

While some irritability is part of normal development, chronic and severe irritability outbursts may signal an underlying mental health condition. Research suggests that irritability in children may be an early predictor of anxiety, depression, or bipolar disorder later in life. Understanding these potential causes can help in addressing , preventing, and managing irritability effectively.
