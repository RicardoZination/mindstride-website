Session 3: Coping with Irritability

Author: Dr. Aroldo Dargél, MD, Ph.D, Chief Medical & Science Officer, Mindstride

Session 3 Overview

In this third session, we explore the resources and tools you have available in your life to help you navigate difficult situations and experiences of irritability. We often call our friends or family to talk about how our day or week has been; do we do the same when we are facing stressful situations? Throughout this session, we also explain different forms of communication that could be helpful in interacting with others and when we are navigating forms of expression when we feel irritated. Let's get started.​

Learning Objectives
  1. Increase your Understanding About Coping with Irritability: Reflect on the effectiveness of what you have done in your past to heighten or calm your feelings of irritation towards someone or something.
  2. Recognize the Role of Proper Communication in Irritability: Increase your awareness of how your tone and speech can help or hinder your ability to express what you are feeling.
  3. Build on Your Communication Skills to Process Irritability: Build on your skills by making informed forms of expression through your daily interactions as you navigate feelings of irritability.
Reflecting on Irritation: A Self-check

Think about a time when you felt irritated.

Was it a specific event? Why was it important to you?

Who was there? What were your initial feelings? 

What did you want to do about it? 

You might have been dealing with a stressful situation beforehand or feeling tired from previous days. What did you do to feel better?

Reflect on how you've coped with difficulties or challenges in the past and evaluate the effectiveness of those strategies

Check in with your resources and skills such as:

  • Friends, Family, Loved Ones
  • Interpersonal Skills
  • Meditation Exercises
  • Hobbies, Physical Activity, etc.
Communication Styles for Managing Irritability

There are three common communication styles people use at different times. Some styles are more effective and appropriate than others, depending on the situation you face.

As you move through this section, think about which style you use most often.

Consider how you communicate in emails, conversations, and even what your body language expresses.
