Understanding Irritability

According to the American Psychological Association, irritability involves excessive and easily triggered anger, annoyance, or impatience. People experiencing irritability tend to be impatient and short-tempered, quickly reacting with frustration to even minor obstacles.

While irritability is a natural human emotion, experiencing it in extremes may indicate an underlying issue that needs to be treated. Persistent and disruptive episodes of irritability can be symptoms of mental health conditions like anxiety or depression, or physical issues such as traumatic brain injury or substance abuse withdrawal.

What is Screen Time?

Screen time is the time spent using electronic media, including social media, watching TV, playing on computers, laptops, smartphones, handheld devices, and video game consoles.​

Balancing Screen Time in the Digital Age​

In this digital age, where devices keep us informed and connected, it's crucial to be mindful of our health and monitor the time we spend on those media. Our devices enable remote work, keep us in touch with friends and family abroad, and help us explore new places and languages. While screen time isn't inherently bad, excessive use can lead to negative health outcomes.

How Much Time Are We Spending On Screens?​

Recent data shows that the average person spends 6.5 hours per day on screens connected to the internet. This time corresponds to 42% of waking hours! Excessive screen time can affect your mood and sleep habits. While screens offer entertainment and connectivity, limiting screen time benefits both physical and mental health.

Finding a balance between screen time and other activities can be challenging and requires adjusting daily habits. Research shows that excessive screen time negatively impacts our brains. So, what is considered excessive?

What is excessive screen time?

Excessive screen time is defined as spending more than 2 hours a day looking at a screen outside of your work hours.

The Impact of Social Media and Screen Time?​​

Social media has become a vital part of everyday communication, facilitating network building, increasing social connections and visibility. Research shows that social media is essential for youth to develop connections in their lives. Conversely, it can also enforce unrealistic expectations and foster comparison, negatively affecting mood and self-esteem. The instant gratification from curated feeds maintains or increases your scrolling activity, while shortening your attention span and increasing symptoms of depression and anxiety.

Time flies when you're scrolling and chatting with friends or family on social media. Whether watching your favorite movie, browsing your feed, or reading the news, hours can pass before you realize it. These hours are often spent sitting or lying down, contributing to a sedentary lifestyle. Research studies report that a sedentary lifestyle is associated with increased levels of depression and anxiety, unhealthy diet, and poor overall health.

​ You may frequently check your daily news updates or notifications, often involving social injustices, accidents, or natural disasters. While staying informed is important, it's equally crucial to find balance for your health and safety. Constant exposure to negative media can trigger feelings of concern and activate your fight-or-flight response, leading to increased anxiety, depressive symptoms, and feelings of isolation.
