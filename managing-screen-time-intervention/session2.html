What is Happening in the Brain During Screen Time?

Studies show that increased screen use among adults may negatively impact learning, memory, and overall mental health. Excessive screen time might reduce the cerebral cortex volume - the brain’s outermost layer responsible for processing memory, decision-making and problem-solving processes.

Watching TV for >= 5 hours per day is associated with increased risk of developing dementia or stroke

Excessive screen time directly impacts your brain’s functioning. Research indicates that adults with high screen time hours or smartphone addiction presented lower gray matter volume.

Gray matter plays a key role in our daily life functioning (e.g., movement control, cognitive and emotional processes). Although gray matter naturally decreases with age, excessive screen time accelerates this reduction.​

While it’s tempting to check overnight notifications and emails, your body isn’t fully awake yet. Research has shown that screen light hyper-activates the nervous system, triggering a fight-or-flight response and increasing stress and anxiety.

To mitigate these effects, replace screen time with healthy behaviors, such as exercise, social activities, and proper sleep.

Screen Time Across the Lifespan​

The Impact of Screen Time on Youth Mental Health​

Use of digital devices has significantly increased among kids and teens in recent years.

The shift to online education and the rise of social media platforms have led to excessive screen time. This excessive screen use is contributing to a decline in mental wellness among teens. For instance, mental health problems now affect 10–20% of young people worldwide.

It is recommended to limit screen time for kids and teens to 2 hours per day.

Research shows that increased screen time is linked to negative self-perception and a higher risk of obesity. The rise of a sedentary lifestyle impacts both adults and youth, as time that could be spent on physical or outdoor activities is often replaced with screen-based entertainment.

Additionally, excessive screen time in youth is associated with poor mental health outcomes, including screen addiction and cyberbullying. Reducing screen time can help mitigate these risks and promote better mental and physical health for kids and teens.

Screen-Based Activities and Health Risks in Adults​

Studying the effects of screen time is challenging due to the prevalence of technology in the workplace. While technology allows adults to work remotely from home or anywhere in the world, it can also interfere with maintaining healthy lifestyle behaviors (e.g., regular sleep ad physical activities). Balancing screen use with healthy daily practices is essential for maintaining overall well-being.

Most of the adult population spends its work hours looking at screens, leading to increased rates of screen-based, sedentary activities. Research shows that increased sedentary time in adults is linked to type 2 diabetes and poor mental health. Reducing sedentary screen time and incorporating physical activity into daily routines can help mitigate these health risks.
