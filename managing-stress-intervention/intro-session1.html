Understanding Stress

Stress is the body's response to any demand that requires adaptation. Our bodies like to maintain balance, known as homeostasis, which is a state of internal stability needed for proper functioning. When stress disrupts this balance, the body works to restore it.

The causes of stress are called stressors. These stressors can be real and physical, like an injury, or they can be perceived or imagined, like worrying about an upcoming event. Understanding both types of stressors helps us better manage our stress levels.

Stress depends on how you perceive what's happening around you and inside your mind. Stressors typically fall into one of two categories: internal or external.

Take a moment to identify your internal and external stressors. List them all and feel free to edit them as you go through this session.

  • Internal Stressors: What thoughts or habits are causing stress within you?
  • External Stressors: What events or situations around you are stressful?

Reflect on these questions:

  • What do you think is causing stress in your life?
  • How does your body react when you think about these stressors?
  • Do you feel happy, excited, nervous, tense, or a combination of emotions?

The Fight or Flight Response

When we face immediate stress, our body's automatic survival mechanism kicks in, known as the fight or flight response. This response prepares us to either confront the stressor or escape from it.

During this response, our body undergoes several changes to help us adapt and overcome the stress. While these physiological changes might feel unsettling, they are essential for helping us manage and respond to immediate threats.​

What is happening in the body?

When our body goes into fight or flight mode, our mind signals immediate danger. This triggers a series of changes:

  • Increased metabolism, heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate: These changes prepare us for quick action.
  • Muscle tension: Often felt in the head, shoulders, and neck.
  • Shortness of breath: Breathing becomes rapid and shallow.
  • Nausea or butterflies in the stomach: A common reaction to stress.

These are normal body responses to stress. Sometimes, people experience a freeze response, where the body feels stuck or in shock due to extreme stress.
