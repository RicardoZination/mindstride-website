Session 4: Preventing and Managing Stress

Author: Dr. Aroldo Dargél, MD, Ph.D, Chief Medical & Science Officer, Mindstride

Session 4 Overview

In Session 4, we focus on preventing and managing everyday stress. Managing stress requires time, patience, and the ability to identify the sources of your stress. Are they external (out of your control) or internal (stemming from your feelings, perceptions, or thoughts)?​

By making lifestyle changes and learning healthy coping strategies, you can enhance your well-being and avoid long-term health complications.​

Learning Objectives
  1. Understand Stress and Its Impact: Learn how to control your response to unavoidable stress and its effects on your health.​
  2. Recognize Different Forms of Stress: Identify signs and symptoms of various stress types to prevent burnout and related conditions.
  3. Adopt Stress Management Habits: Build a personalized toolkit to manage stress, enhancing your well-being through better thinking, nutrition, exercise, and social support.
Well-being in Routine​

What does a typical day in your life look like? For example, you might start with a cup of coffee, get ready for work, commute, complete tasks at your job, run errands like going to the bank or grocery shopping, and then return home for dinner and a favorite show. Alternatively, your daily routine might vary, with each day looking different.

When we have a routine, we can set expectations and contingency plans if things don’t go as planned. This is harder to do when your day is unpredictable. The Power of Self-Talk in Managing Stress

What we say to ourselves greatly impacts our ability to handle stress. For example, when things don't go as planned, do you think, "Oh no, this is the worst!" or "Don't worry, I've got this"?

Positive thinking can reduce stress by fostering a compassionate and understanding self-dialogue.​

Self-affirmations are also effective in shifting your mindset. By giving yourself support and encouragement, rather than being harsh or judgmental, you can better manage the stress in your life.​

The Benefits of Movement in Reducing Stress

Any form of movement during the day can help your body shift towards relaxation rather than stress.

Regular exercise, even just 15 minutes a day, can balance your stress load. It could be a short walk or a long hike, whatever fits your schedule.​

Set a daily movement goal. Choose activities that bring you joy, like going out with friends, gardening, biking, or weight training. Listen to your body as you exercise.

Start with what you're comfortable with, and gradually expand your range when you feel ready.

Social Support is Key in Managing Stress

Leaning on your social support network during stressful times can help you navigate and discuss what's causing your stress. Your support system may be able to help, preventing you from feeling alone in your stress.

Who can you talk to during these times? Is it family, a particular friend, a loved one, or a trusted colleague? Identify someone you can vent to or find solutions with. Do you have regular contact with them? Do they also reach out to you in times of stress?​

Consider others beyond your current support network. Reach out and have a chat—it may help reduce your stress load and make practical decisions easier.

Eat Well to Control Stress

A balanced meal, especially during stressful times, can set a positive tone for the rest of your day. While it's okay to enjoy a treat, remember that it alone isn't a full meal and may even increase stress symptoms.

Here are some suggestions for balanced meals:

  • Stay hydrated: Drink plenty of water throughout the day.
  • Avoid excessive sugar and caffeine: These can spike stress levels.
  • Snack smartly: Opt for nuts, fruits, or yogurt instead of processed snacks.

Eating well can help you manage stress and maintain your overall health.

Wrap-up

Stress plays a major role in our lives, pushing us to meet new challenges or causing worry about deadlines. It affects how we act, think, and feel. While stress can boost energy and productivity, it can also lead to fatigue and burnout. By adapting our perceptions of stress, we can reduce its negative impacts on our bodies.
