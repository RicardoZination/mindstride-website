Session 3: Recognizing Stress in your Life

Author: Dr. Aroldo Dargél, MD, Ph.D, Chief Medical & Science Officer, Mindstride

Session Summary

In this third session, we will build your capacity to appraise the good and bad stress that may appear in your life. Some forms of stress may boost your productivity and keep you going, while the other can do the opposite and set negative mental health outcomes that take a toll on your overall health. In this session we invite you to complete the worksheet to get to know how much stress may be affecting your quality of life.

Learning Objectives
  1. Understand the Role of the Stressors and Their Impact: Learn how to control your response to everyday stressors and the levels of stress they may bring.
  2. Recognize the Stressors in Your Daily Life: Identify the different types of stressors that are present in your day-to-day activities that may be impacting your mental health.
  3. Identify the Difference in Good and Bad Stressors: Build a personalized toolkit to manage stress, enhancing your well-being by identifying stressful situations and lifestyle factors in your life.

Appraising Stressors in your Daily Life​

Stress impacts how our body works, how we act, think, and feel. When stressed, our bodies often become tense and enter the 'fight or flight' mode. Our responses can vary based on our personality and circumstances.

Now that we've identified whether stressors are internal or external, let's determine if they bring good stress (eustress) or bad stress (distress) into our lives. Use the Stress Appraisal table below to categorize the causes of your stress as either eustress or distress.​

Notice how your mind appraises each stressor. Is it a fun challenge, an exciting adventure, or a thrilling road trip? This is eustress. Or is it a dreadful task or a frustrating discussion? This is distress. Remember, your feelings may not always be clear-cut—you can feel multiple ways about a situation, and that’s completely okay!​

Using the right side of the table, give each item a score from 1 to 10, with 1 being the lowest possible level of stress it causes you today and 10 being the highest.

​Practice this exercise for two weeks while applying the techniques to prevent and manage stress from the next session. We will then discuss any changes in your original scores from Day 1.​
